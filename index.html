JourneyUp · 2025–Present Design + Front-end AI / Travel · Pre-launch

JourneyUp — a voice-first travel concierge that guides instead of overwhelms

What it is. JourneyUp is a voice-first AI travel app: you say where you want to go, and it builds a personalized route — stops, hotels, points of interest — without making you do the organizing work. I'm the designer and one of the front-end engineers. There's no separate handoff process; I design directly in the React codebase, making decisions at the component level and the copy level in the same workflow. The product is currently in investor demo stage.

The central design problem. A travel planning tool lives or dies on one question: how much do you hand back to the user? Show them every option and you've rebuilt the problem they came to solve. Show them nothing and you haven't earned their trust. My position was guide-first: the app makes a call, shows one route, and makes changing it feel like adjusting a plan — not overriding a system.

Decision: route view. The itinerary shows stops in the order we recommend. Users don't choose from alternatives — they get a route. But two escape hatches are always available: drag to reorder any stop by touch, or tell the app to move it by voice. The same action is available both ways. For a voice-first product, nothing critical should be touch-only.

Decision: hotel carousel. Accommodation options appear inline as a swipeable card carousel inside the route list, not on a separate screen. The intent was to keep the user inside the planning flow — browsing hotels shouldn't feel like a detour.

Decision: onboarding scope. Early versions collected demographic data — age, gender — that the AI wasn't actually using. I cut it. What stayed: vehicle make, model, and year, which directly improves route accuracy for range, charging stops, and road type. The rule became: only ask what earns its place.

Process. I use Claude Code as a design collaborator — describing what feels wrong, getting analysis, making the call, pushing the PR. Design decisions happen at the token level (the full theme system), the component level (information hierarchy on hotel cards, mic toggle accessibility), and the copy level ("Ready when you are" instead of "idle"). It's a tighter loop than any Figma-first process I've worked in.

Cisco · 2022–2026 Lead UX + Full-stack Enterprise Cloud

Cisco's internal cloud platforms — leading UX across maturity stages

Context. I was the lead designer on two of Cisco's internal cloud-management platforms: RunOn Managed Service (RMS), a mature platform broadly deployed across the company, and Control Plane Platform (CPP), its newer, faster-growing counterpart. My work spanned research, Figma prototyping, React implementation, and ongoing support — partnered with PMs, engineers, and two designers I guided, including collaboration with design and engineering counterparts in India and China.

Scale. RMS manages tens of thousands of compute and storage resources (VMs, storage, AWS accounts) across hundreds of business applications, handling tens of thousands of resource requests from thousands of distinct users every quarter. CPP is earlier in its lifecycle but already serving thousands of quarterly users — a signal it's finding adoption fast.

The design problem I cared about most. Two products in the same domain at very different maturity stages. RMS users had deep muscle memory and high tolerance for complexity; CPP users were new and needed onboarding that didn't bury them. The job was to maintain enough consistency that the platforms felt like one ecosystem, while letting each speak to its own audience.

A representative project: the Platform Selection experience. The pivotal feature for both products is where users — software engineers and system administrators — choose the underlying cloud platform for their infrastructure (AWS, GCP, and others). It demanded a careful balance between simplicity and comprehensiveness: enough information for an informed decision, not so much that users got buried. I designed a card-based grid layout where each card carried the essentials — provider logo, tags, a short description, and a link to documentation — and nothing more. The grid prevents information overload and supports quick scanning, while the per-card detail link gives power users a deeper path when they need it.

Outcome. Usability testing affirmed the design's intuitiveness and efficiency, and the pattern became part of the platforms' broader design language. More broadly, both products continue to serve thousands of engineers across Cisco — and the design system I helped shape is the substrate they're built on.

Stack: React · Go · Python · Figma  ·  Role: Lead designer across both products · guided 2 designers · cross-functional with PMs, engineering, and global teams · shipped production code
CData Software · 2019–2021 Front-end + UI Design B2B SaaS

CData — making a catalog of 100+ data sources legible

Context. CData sells a portfolio of enterprise data connectivity products. I owned design and front-end implementation for much of the marketing site and worked directly with the product team on UI for multiple connectivity platform applications.

Problem. CData's "Drivers" surface had to present over 100 diverse data sources — social platforms, database systems, file formats — across platforms like ADO.net. The default approach (long lists, undifferentiated entries) buried users in choice and forced cognitive load onto people who just wanted to find the connector they needed.

Approach. I designed a user-centric grid layout that displayed each data source with just enough detail to recognize it, paired with interactive "pill" filters for categorization. Filter interactions were powered by relational database updates on the back end and smoothed in the front end with lightweight JavaScript animations, so the experience felt responsive without being noisy. The whole pattern was modular by design — easy for the team to extend as new connectors were added.

Outcome. The design became a cornerstone of the CData site and is still in use, influencing purchasing decisions by making the catalog navigable rather than overwhelming. It's the kind of work I'm proudest of: a small interface that quietly does a lot of work for the user.

Stack: HTML5 · CSS3 · JavaScript · SASS · Bootstrap  ·  Role: Designed and coded most of the site
